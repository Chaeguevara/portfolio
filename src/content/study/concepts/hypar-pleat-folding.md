# Pleat Folding & the Hyperbolic Paraboloid

*(한국어: [플리트 접기와 쌍곡포물면 (Hypar)](/portfolio/study/hypar-pleat-folding.ko/))*

> The classic concentric-square 'hypar' model provably cannot fold without extra creases — straight creases stay straight and flat polygons stay flat.

## Idea
The famous origami **hyperbolic paraboloid (hypar)** — concentric square pleats
plus diagonals, from the 1920s Bauhaus — turns out **not to exist** as a folding of
that crease pattern. The course proves this with a theory of how paper behaves
*between* creases.

## Key results (how paper bends between creases)
- A flat (uncreased) polygonal region of paper must stay **flat** in 3D.
- A **straight crease stays straight** when folded.
- Therefore concentric squares + diagonals alone cannot form a curved surface —
  contradiction, so the idealized hypar can't fold. Adding specific diagonal
  creases to each trapezoidal ring *does* make it fold.

## Why it matters
It is a clean cautionary tale: a model everyone "folds" is mathematically
impossible with the drawn creases. The same between-crease theory underlies
**curved-crease folding** (e.g. reconstructing David Huffman's sculptures) and
connects to [Rigid Origami](/portfolio/study/rigid-origami/) and self-folding materials.
